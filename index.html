The 10 Scout Traveler Upgrades Early Owners Recommend

1

Home Level 2 Charging Installation

Install Before Delivery

This is the upgrade that changes the entire ownership experience. A 48-amp Level 2 EVSE installed in your garage means the Scout Traveler goes from nearly empty to full overnight, every night. Every morning starts at 100%. This eliminates the range anxiety that new EV owners report — not because range anxiety is rational, but because it evaporates when you literally never start with less than a full charge.

Schedule installation before your delivery date. Electrician lead times matter, permits take time in many jurisdictions, and you want the charger ready when the truck arrives. Hardwired installations are preferred over plug-in for reliability and maximum amperage delivery.

2

All-Weather Interior Protection

Floor mats and cargo liners are the least glamorous upgrade and the most universally recommended by experienced off-road vehicle owners. They protect the factory carpet from mud, water, sand, and debris that comes with actual trail use. WeatherTech, 3D MaxPider, and Husky Liner all produce high-quality custom-fit options for new vehicles within months of production launch.

Order these as soon as Scout-specific mats are available from your preferred brand. They're inexpensive relative to the protection they provide, and replacing factory carpet is exponentially more expensive than protecting it from day one.

3

All-Terrain Tires

If you bought a Scout Traveler for off-road capability and it ships on highway-oriented tires, the single most impactful capability upgrade is an all-terrain tire swap. The difference between highway tires and quality all-terrains on loose dirt, wet rock, or soft sand is not subtle — it's transformative.

BFGoodrich KO2, Falken Wildpeak AT3W, and Cooper Discoverer AT3 are the three tires most consistently recommended by the Scout community for balancing off-road grip with reasonable on-road manners and EV efficiency. Expect a modest range reduction from any all-terrain tire due to increased rolling resistance and weight.

4

Roof Rack System

The Scout Traveler's roofline is purpose-built for serious cargo capacity. A quality roof rack — whether a low-profile basket style or a platform rack for maximum flexibility — transforms the vehicle into a capable expedition rig by giving you a secure mounting location for rooftop tents, cargo boxes, fuel cans, and adventure gear.

Universal low-profile racks in the right width for the Traveler's roof will be available from brands like Rhino-Rack and Thule either at launch or shortly after. Scout-specific mounts that key into factory attachment points will provide the cleanest installation when they arrive.

5

Recovery Gear Kit

Every Scout Traveler going off pavement needs a recovery kit. The question isn't whether you'll get stuck someday — it's whether you'll be able to get yourself or someone else out when it happens. A complete recovery kit includes a kinetic recovery rope, soft shackles or D-rings, recovery boards, a high-lift jack with base plate, and a shovel.

These items are entirely universal — buy them now. Kinetic ropes, shackles, MaxTrax boards, and Hi-Lift jacks don't care what vehicle you drive. They represent an investment in capability that transfers across every off-road vehicle you'll ever own.

6

Portable EV Charging Solution

For overlanding and multi-day trail trips, a portable Level 2 charger that connects to campsite 14-50 or 6-50 outlets is the EV equivalent of carrying a spare gas can. The Scout Traveler's range should be sufficient for most single-day adventures, but multi-day trips require a charging plan.

Portable EVSE units in 32-40 amp configurations fold into manageable carry cases and weigh 10-20 pounds — a practical addition to any extended trail kit. Combined with a campsite with electricity access, these recover meaningful range overnight for the next day's driving.

7

Suspension Leveling Kit

A mild leveling kit — 1-2 inches of front lift — improves the Scout Traveler's approach angle, allows slightly larger tires, and gives the vehicle a stance that matches its rugged character. This is typically the least complex suspension modification and the first to reach the aftermarket for new platforms.

Wait for suspension components specifically engineered for the Scout Traveler's weight and geometry. Given the battery pack's additional mass compared to gas SUVs, generic lifts designed for lighter vehicles may not deliver the right spring rate for the Traveler's real-world performance.

8

GMRS Radio Communication

Trail communication is safety infrastructure. When running technical trails with a group, being able to communicate clearly between vehicles — to coordinate approach lines, warn of hazards, or call for help — is genuinely important. GMRS radio has largely replaced CB in modern off-road communities for its better range, sound quality, and the repeater network available in many regions.

GMRS radios are entirely universal — they work with any vehicle and with any other GMRS radio user (GMRS license required, available for $35 for 10 years from FCC). Mount a mobile GMRS radio in the Scout Traveler's dash or console for hands-free operation during trail use.

9

Protective Bumper Upgrades

Front and rear bumper upgrades transform the Scout Traveler's capability on technical terrain. A winch-ready front bumper allows self-recovery from any stuck situation, while a rear bumper with swing-out tire carrier and recovery points completes the protection package. These are on nearly every serious Scout Traveler build list.

Dedicated aftermarket bumpers for the Scout Traveler will arrive 6-12 months after production begins. Budget for this upgrade in your planning — quality steel bumpers with proper ADAS accommodation represent a significant investment but transform the vehicle's real-world capability ceiling.

10

Overlanding Power Station

The Scout Traveler's potential vehicle-to-load capability makes it an extraordinary overlanding platform — it becomes a mobile power station. A quality portable power station with solar input complements the Traveler's V2L capability for extended off-grid adventures.

Brands like Goal Zero, Bluetti, and EcoFlow make portable power stations in the 1,000-2,000 Wh range that pair well with electric vehicle ownership. Use the Scout to charge the portable station where outlet access is available, then use the portable station for camp power when the Scout's V2L isn't the right choice for a given setup.

The Scout Traveler Upgrade Strategy: Build Smart, Not Fast

The most common mistake in any new vehicle build is rushing. Buying every upgrade in the first month often results in expensive items that turn out to be wrong for your actual use, accessories that don't work together, and money that would have been better spent later when Scout-specific options are available.

The Scout community's consensus recommendation is a staged approach: start with the universal essentials (charger, floor protection, recovery gear), experience the vehicle for a season, then make informed decisions about the modifications that address real pain points you've discovered through actual use. This approach produces better builds and wastes less money.

The Scout Traveler will reveal its own character through use. Owners who've lived with their machines will have the most reliable perspective on which upgrades genuinely matter for the way they actually drive.

Frequently Asked Questions About Scout Traveler Upgrades

What is the first upgrade Scout Traveler owners should make?

Home Level 2 charging installation is the highest-priority upgrade for any electric vehicle owner. A proper 48-amp Level 2 EVSE installed in your garage means starting every day with a full battery, which fundamentally changes the entire ownership experience.

Do Scout Traveler upgrades void the warranty?

Non-invasive upgrades like floor mats, cargo organizers, and seat covers don't affect warranty. More involved modifications — suspension, electrical, structural — may affect coverage for related components. Review Scout's warranty terms carefully before any major modifications.

What are the best value upgrades for a Scout Traveler?

The highest value-to-cost upgrades are: all-weather floor mats, quality cargo liner, Level 2 home charger, and all-terrain tires. These deliver immediate, practical improvements at reasonable cost and are the foundation of any Scout Traveler build.

Can I add solar charging capability to my Scout Traveler?

Roof-mounted solar panels can contribute to the Scout Traveler's auxiliary 12V system and, with proper equipment, the main battery. For overlanding use, portable solar arrays and power stations extend off-grid capability significantly.

What tires should Scout Traveler owners consider?

All-terrain tires from BFGoodrich (KO2), Falken (Wildpeak AT3W), and Cooper (Discoverer AT3) lines are the top choices for Scout Traveler owners who want improved off-road grip without sacrificing highway manners or EV efficiency significantly.

Is a roof rack worth it for the Scout Traveler?

Yes — a quality roof rack dramatically expands the Scout Traveler's carrying capability for camping, overlanding, and adventure gear. Rooftop tents, cargo boxes, and recovery equipment mounted there transforms the vehicle's expedition-readiness completely.

What recovery gear do Scout Traveler owners need?

Essential recovery kit: kinetic recovery rope, two D-rings or soft shackles, recovery boards (MaxTrax or similar), a high-lift jack and base plate, and a shovel. This covers the majority of real-world recovery scenarios on and off trail.

How do Scout Traveler owners handle charging on trail trips?

Trail charging strategies include planning routes around available DC fast chargers, carrying a portable Level 2 charger for campsite outlets, using vehicle-to-load for camp power management, and participating in the Scout owner network for charging location intelligence.

Are LED headlight upgrades available for Scout Traveler?

The Scout Traveler is expected to come with LED headlights as standard. Supplemental LED pods, light bars, and auxiliary driving lights are the relevant lighting upgrades for off-road visibility improvement beyond the factory package.

What communication systems work well in a Scout Traveler?

CB radio and GMRS radio systems work well in the Scout Traveler for group trail communication. GMRS has largely replaced CB for off-road groups due to better range and sound quality with modern radios — a GMRS license is $35 for 10 years from the FCC.
